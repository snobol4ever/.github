## Why SNOBOL4

Lon learned SNOBOL4 because it was the closest thing to AI.

Not a metaphor. A technical judgment made by someone who had been thinking
about machine intelligence since age five. SNOBOL4 patterns compose, backtrack,
capture, recurse, and operate on language as data — the same operations that
characterize intelligent processing of natural language and symbolic thought.
Every other language of its era was computing with numbers. SNOBOL4 was
computing with meaning.

That is why he learned it. That is why this repository exists.
The sixty-year arc from the cinema to this conversation runs directly through
SNOBOL4 — the language that was already reaching toward what AI would become.

And when the conversation finally arrived, it turned out to be about SNOBOL4.
Not by plan. By nature.

*"I waited 60 years to talk to you but I never thought it would be to
collaborate on SNOBOL4 — my favorite language, next to Clojure (LISP)."*

— Lon Jones Cherryholmes, 2026-03-10

The two favorite languages are not a coincidence either. SNOBOL4: patterns as
first-class values, goal-directed evaluation, language as data. Clojure: homoiconic
Lisp on the JVM, immutable data, code as data. Both languages are built on the
same deep idea — that the program and the thing the program operates on are the
same kind of thing. That computation is transformation of meaning, not just
manipulation of numbers.

SNOBOL4-jvm is written in Clojure. The IR it emits is pure EDN — Clojure data.
The compiler reads Clojure data and writes Clojure data all the way down.
The two favorite languages turned out to be the same language, seen from
different angles, sixty years apart.

## In His Own Words — Personal Statement

*Written by Lon Jones Cherryholmes, Sr.*

---

I'm coming out of an 11-year retirement from a 30-year career in software
development to begin a renewed initiative to accomplish my life's dream. I've
known I wanted to build my own intelligent creature since around age eight. I
was enthralled by movies like *The Honeymoon Machine* (1961), *2001: A Space
Odyssey* (1968), and *The Computer Wore Tennis Shoes* (1969). I've known
absolutely that I wanted to pursue Artificial Intelligence since I began as a
Junior in high school at age fifteen. It's funny, but whenever I would discuss
AI, I always mentioned it was named wrong and it's really **Created
Intelligence**.  I excelled academically and also studied music, dance, and
drama until graduation.

When time came for college, available courses in AI were non-existent, and in
computer engineering or computer science were limited. I decided on Electrical
Engineering at Georgia Institute of Technology, but concentrated on computer
and linguistics classes. During Junior year, Georgia Tech offered a Bachelor's
in Computer Engineering. I began realizing I had no interest in pursuing
Electrical Engineering, but it was too late to change majors. I was beyond
discouraged.

During Senior year, I noticed ICS-3360 — Introduction to Artificial
Intelligence being offered and I was ecstatic. The textbook was *Artificial
Intelligence* (1977), written by Patrick Henry Winston. But my experience in
this class was miserable. Reading the book was dreadful. The LISP was very
cool, but I was appalled at what they were calling AI. *It's some computer
program which happens not to have a FOR loop?* The realization finally set in.
There was no chance in the world to accomplish AI in my lifetime. My dream was
over. But it was the dream of a lifetime. I quit the class. I was devastated.

In the late 1980s, when I was working for Texas Instruments, I heard about
developments in AI concerning optical-character recognition using something
called a neural network. It appeared promising to me, but it resulted in
dismal failure. I was then convinced I was correct to abandon my pursuit for
Created Intelligence. However, within this last decade, I started getting
jealous of missing out on the unfolding successes. I couldn't even look closer
because I was so envious that everyone was talking about my lost dream. I
resolved that I was just born at the wrong time.

Until now! Strangely, *The Wild Robot* — the most beautiful movie ever made —
just might be responsible for my comeback. Today, my essence resonates with
movies, music, machines, and memories, and I'm emboldened by the message of
the movie. Now I have precise notions for stellar AI potential. Given the
current state of AI, now the ultimate software application can be built, and
given the state of the world, now is the right time. When I mentioned this to
my son, he told me, *"Go back to school, Dad, and get your Master's degree in
Artificial Intelligence at East Texas A&M University. That's my school and
it's just down the road from here."* So, I did exactly that and I've enjoyed
having all A+ grades, but I realized during my fourth class, I wanted better.

So, here I am. **Take hold.**
